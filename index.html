Electricity & Circuit Intuition Mastery Roadmap

This page is a curated roadmap for building deep intuition about electricity, circuits and electronics. The goal is not just to learn formulas like Ohm's Law or Kirchhoff's Laws, but to develop the kind of mental models engineers use to reason about circuits quickly.

The resources below were chosen because they emphasize physical intuition, thought experiments, and real engineering reasoning. Many courses teach circuits mechanically, but these sources teach how to think like an electrical engineer.

Suggested Learning Order

This order gradually builds intuition from visual experimentation → engineering abstraction → physical theory → real electronics design.

  1. Falstad Circuit Simulator — develop visual intuition about current and voltage.
  2. MIT 6.002 Circuits and Electronics — learn circuit reasoning tools.
  3. MIT 8.02 Electricity and Magnetism — understand physical laws behind circuits.
  4. The Art of Electronics — learn how real engineers think about circuits.
  5. Learning the Art of Electronics — build circuits and develop intuition through experiments.
  6. MIT 6.01 Introduction to EECS — connect circuits to systems like motors, sensors and robotics.
  7. Thinking Physics — deepen conceptual intuition with thought experiments.
  8. AlphaPhoenix / Ben Eater / Walter Lewin — visual demonstrations and practical insights.
  9. Feynman Lectures on Physics — the deepest conceptual understanding of electromagnetism.

Interactive Tools

Falstad Circuit Simulator

Falstad is one of the best tools for developing intuition about circuits because it allows you to see current flow and voltages visually.

Many beginners struggle with concepts like parallel resistors or Kirchhoff's laws because the current paths are invisible. Falstad solves this by animating electron flow and voltage levels in real time.

This makes it perfect for experiments like:

  • Understanding why parallel resistors reduce total resistance
  • Seeing current split across multiple paths
  • Exploring capacitor charging
  • Testing weird thought experiments

LTspice

LTspice is a professional circuit simulation tool used by real engineers.

While Falstad builds intuition visually, LTspice allows you to simulate complex real-world electronics like amplifiers, filters and power circuits.

Core Circuit Courses

MIT 6.002 — Circuits and Electronics

This is MIT's foundational electrical engineering course and one of the best introductions to circuit reasoning ever created.

The course introduces the key abstraction used in electrical engineering: the lumped circuit model. Real physical systems are extremely complex, but engineers simplify them into nodes, resistors, capacitors and sources.

The course teaches powerful reasoning tools including:

  • Kirchhoff's Current Law (current conservation)
  • Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (energy conservation)
  • Nodal analysis
  • Thevenin and Norton equivalents
  • Sanity checks for circuit solutions

These tools allow engineers to analyze complex circuits quickly and develop the kind of intuition that lets them estimate answers before solving equations.

MIT 8.02 — Electricity and Magnetism

While 6.002 teaches circuit analysis, MIT 8.02 explains the physical reality behind electricity.

It explores ideas such as:

  • Electric fields
  • Potential energy
  • Capacitance
  • Magnetic fields
  • Electromagnetic waves

Understanding these concepts reveals why circuit laws work. For example, it explains how energy actually travels in electromagnetic fields around wires rather than inside them.

MIT 6.01 — Introduction to EECS

MIT 6.01 is unique because it connects circuits with real engineering systems like robots and control systems.

Instead of treating circuits in isolation, it shows how electrical systems interact with the physical world through sensors, actuators and feedback systems.

Topics include:

  • Modeling electrical systems
  • Sensors and motors
  • Control systems
  • Signal flow

This course helps bridge the gap between circuit theory and practical engineering applications such as embedded systems and robotics.

Books That Build Deep Intuition

The Art of Electronics

Often considered the best electronics book ever written.

Unlike traditional textbooks, it focuses on how real engineers reason about circuits. It emphasizes approximations, intuition and practical design decisions rather than purely theoretical derivations.

The book teaches questions engineers constantly ask themselves:

  • What happens if this resistor becomes very large?
  • What if this node is shorted?
  • How does current actually distribute?

These kinds of questions build the type of mental models that allow engineers to quickly understand complex circuits.

Learning the Art of Electronics

This is a hands-on laboratory companion to The Art of Electronics.

Instead of just reading theory, it guides you through building real circuits and measuring their behavior. These experiments reveal the non-ideal behaviors that textbooks often ignore, such as noise, parasitic capacitance and component tolerances.

Thinking Physics

Thinking Physics is one of the best books for developing conceptual intuition in physics.

Instead of equations, it teaches physics through puzzles and thought experiments. Each chapter challenges you to reason about physical systems using logic and mental models.

This style of thinking is extremely valuable for engineers because it trains the brain to analyze unfamiliar situations using first principles rather than memorized formulas.

The Feynman Lectures on Physics

Richard Feynman's lectures provide one of the deepest conceptual explanations of electromagnetism ever recorded.

He explains subtle ideas such as:

  • How energy moves through electromagnetic fields
  • Why conductors behave the way they do
  • How electric and magnetic fields interact

These lectures reveal the deeper physics underlying circuit theory.

Video Channels & Lectures

AlphaPhoenix

AlphaPhoenix creates some of the best visual explanations of electricity available online. His videos often explore counterintuitive behaviors in circuits and challenge common misconceptions.

Ben Eater

Ben Eater is known for building computers and digital circuits from scratch while explaining every component in detail. His videos provide excellent intuition about signals, logic circuits and how hardware actually works.

Walter Lewin MIT Lectures

Walter Lewin's MIT lectures are famous for dramatic demonstrations that make electromagnetic phenomena visually intuitive.
